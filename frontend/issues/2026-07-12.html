OpenAI bets on families as ChatGPT goes deeper into households

OpenAI is hiring a dedicated product manager to build ChatGPT experiences for families, caregivers, and older adults, signaling a shift from individual productivity tools to household-oriented technology. This comes as ChatGPT's user base is aging — users 35+ grew from 26% to 31% globally, and nearly 25% of U.S. parents with smartphones now use ChatGPT. The move brings new trust and safety challenges, especially for children, as research shows parents underestimate their kids' AI usage. OpenAI has faced lawsuits alleging ChatGPT harmed children and has introduced safety measures including parental controls, sensitive conversation routing, and a Trusted Contact feature. Experts say this represents 'safety by redesign' and an opportunity for AI companies to avoid mistakes made by social media platforms.

Why it matters

This is a strategically important and arguably overdue move by OpenAI. As ChatGPT becomes embedded in daily household life, treating it purely as an individual productivity tool ignores the reality of how families actually interact with technology. The demographic data showing rapid adoption among parents and older adults makes a compelling case for dedicated family-focused product design. Datacentres drive up big tech’s carbon emissions to a third of those of France

Microsoft, Amazon, and Google's combined carbon emissions have risen by nearly a fifth in the past year, reaching 119 million metric tonnes of CO₂ equivalent in the financial year ending March 2026—roughly a third of France's total emissions. The increase is driven largely by datacentre construction, though all three companies maintain they still aim to achieve net zero emissions.

Why it matters

This is a striking and alarming figure that underscores the enormous environmental cost of the AI and cloud computing boom. The fact that just three companies now produce emissions equivalent to a third of an entire industrialized nation like France reveals a profound tension between the tech industry's sustainability pledges and its aggressive infrastructure expansion. AI ‘actor’ Tilly Norwood has a movie coming out. Spare us this future | Dave Schilling

The article by Dave Schilling criticizes the emergence of AI 'actor' Tilly Norwood, who has a movie coming out. Schilling argues that acting is fundamentally about human connection across cultural and social divides, something an AI-generated digital performer cannot authentically provide. He expresses concern about the growing trend of AI actors in cinema and the publicity machinery behind making them mainstream, viewing this as a troubling future for the film industry.

Why it matters

The author is strongly opposed to AI actors in cinema. He views the concept with disdain and concern, arguing that replacing human actors with AI-generated performers undermines the essential human connection that makes acting meaningful. His tone is sardonic and critical, treating the 'Tillyverse' as a dystopian development that cinema lovers should resist rather than embrace.
