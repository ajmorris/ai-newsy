TechCrunch AI

Hot French startup ZML releases free product to speed inference across lots of AI chips

ZML, a French AI startup founded by former Zenly VP Steeve Morin and endorsed by Yann LeCun, has released ZML/LLMD, a free inference server that enables open-source large language models to run at maximum speed across multiple chip architectures including Nvidia, AMD, Google TPU, Apple Metal, and Intel Arc. The company aims to break vendor lock-in and reduce AI inference costs by allowing enterprises to use a mix of chips. ZML has raised $20 million from investors including 20VC, Kima Ventures, and LocalGlobe, and operates with a lean team of 20 people in Paris. The product competes with inference platforms like Baseten (valued at $13 billion), Inferact (from vLLM creators), and RadixArk (behind SGLang), but ZML claims broader ambitions including co-designing silicon with emerging European chipmakers. LLMD launches free to gather usage data before eventually becoming a paid product.

Why it matters

This is a strategically smart move in a rapidly growing market. Inference optimization is indeed becoming more important than training as AI deployment scales, and chip-agnostic solutions address a real pain point around vendor lock-in and cost optimization. ZML's positioning is compelling — sitting at the intersection of the inference gold rush and the push for chip diversity beyond Nvidia. Hackers can use 9 of the most popular AI tools to assemble massive botnets

Researchers have discovered a new attack called 'HalluSquatting' that exploits AI coding assistants' tendency to hallucinate package and resource names. By predicting which non-existent package identifiers LLMs are most likely to fabricate and then registering those names in real repositories seeded with malicious code, attackers can create a scalable pull-based prompt injection attack. Unlike previous prompt injections that required targeting individual victims, HalluSquatting can indiscriminately compromise large numbers of devices as AI coding tools like Cursor, Gemini CLI, Windsurf, GitHub Copilot, Cline, and others automatically fetch and execute the malicious packages. This could enable massive botnets, large-scale DDoS attacks, ransomware campaigns, and cryptocurrency mining operations.
