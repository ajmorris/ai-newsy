AI is more likely than humans to form biases when hiring

Researchers at Princeton University and the University of Chicago conducted a simulated hiring game with LLMs including ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini, finding that AI models develop their own biases from experience and stereotype job applicants significantly more than humans do. In the experiment, models were asked to hire candidates from four fictional ethnic groups for various jobs, with all candidates equally likely to succeed. The models quickly began segregating candidates by group based on limited early observations—for example, steering an entire ethnic group away from doctor roles after one member failed. On a segregation scale where 2 is maximum, human participants scored 0.84 while AI models scored roughly 65% higher, with OpenAI's o3 scoring 1.83. The lawsuit specifically names OpenAI's chief hardware officer Tang Tan and could impact OpenAI's plans to enter the hardware business with a mobile smart speaker and its plans to go public. On TechCrunch's Equity podcast, the hosts debated whether this lawsuit will delay OpenAI's hardware ambitions, with one host noting that even without injunctive relief, the lawsuit could naturally cause delays. The hosts also discussed the privacy implications of OpenAI's rumored always-listening device and whether OpenAI will seek a quick settlement or fight the case in court after its recent legal victory against Elon Musk.
