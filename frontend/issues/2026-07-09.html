Google’s deepfake detector system used to debunk McConnell hoax pic

Google's SynthID watermark system successfully debunked a viral AI-generated hoax image purporting to show Senator Mitch McConnell in extreme distress in a hospital bed. The fake image circulated widely on Reddit and X before Snopes verified it contained the SynthID watermark, confirming it was AI-generated. SynthID, launched at Google I/O 2025, embeds an invisible signature into AI-generated images that survives screencaptures across platforms. The system's main limitation is that it only works when image-generation tools participate; Gemini models include it since 2025, OpenAI joined in May 2026, but Anthropic does not participate. Users can verify images through Gemini or OpenAI's public verification tool.

Why it matters

This is a genuinely encouraging development for AI safety and content authenticity. The fact that SynthID worked exactly as intended in a high-profile, politically charged scenario demonstrates that invisible watermarking can be a practical tool against deepfakes. However, the article rightly highlights the critical limitation: the system only works when AI providers opt in. Anthropic's non-participation is a notable gap. This startup thinks robotics is about to have its ChatGPT moment

General Intuition, led by CEO Pim de Witte, has raised $320 million at a $2.3 billion valuation for its thesis that robotics is approaching a foundation-model moment similar to what ChatGPT did for NLP. The startup trains its foundation model on millions of hours of video game data—including human controller inputs—to develop spatial-temporal reasoning. Rather than requiring massive real-world datasets, the company claims its model can be fine-tuned with just minutes of real-world robotics data. It demonstrated a quadrupedal robot operating with only eight minutes of fine-tuning data using a single front camera. General Intuition's goal is not to build robots itself but to become the base foundation model for physical AI that other robotics companies build upon, much like GPT serves as a starting point for NLP applications.

Why it matters

This is a compelling and ambitious thesis, and the video game data angle is genuinely clever—game environments provide rich, diverse spatial-temporal interaction data with precise action labels at a scale that's nearly impossible to replicate in the real world. The demo of a quadrupedal robot working with just eight minutes of fine-tuning is impressive if it holds up under scrutiny. However, there are reasons for skepticism.
