What the Fight With Anthropic Reveals About Free Speech in America

The article discusses a legal or public dispute involving Anthropic and argues that the debate over AI regulation is fundamentally tied to how society conceptualizes coding as a form of free speech. It contends that meaningful AI regulation will remain elusive until there is a shift in how the right to write and distribute code is understood within the framework of First Amendment protections in America.

Why it matters

The article appears to take the position that current free speech frameworks are inadequate for addressing the challenges posed by AI, and that treating code purely as protected speech hinders necessary regulation. The author seems to advocate for rethinking the legal and cultural status of code so that more effective AI governance can be implemented, suggesting that the fight with Anthropic exemplifies broader tensions between technological freedom and public accountability.

Apple takes OpenAI to court

Apple has filed a lawsuit against OpenAI, its hardware chief Tang Tan, and its io devices unit, alleging that OpenAI systematically poached over 400 Apple employees and used them as a pipeline for stealing confidential hardware trade secrets. Apple claims Tang Tan, who spent 24 years at Apple before joining OpenAI, conducted interviews where candidates were told to bring 'actual parts,' and that ex-iPhone engineer Chang Liu exploited a bug to access confidential files after joining OpenAI. Apple is asking the court to force a redesign of OpenAI's unreleased Jony Ive-designed hardware device expected in 2027. OpenAI responded by saying it has 'no interest in other companies' trade secrets.' Apple says it flagged the suspected theft in a February letter and received no response.

Why it matters

This lawsuit represents a dramatic escalation in the deteriorating relationship between Apple and OpenAI, two companies that were recently partners on Siri integration. The allegations are quite serious — if true, the claim that a hardware chief instructed interview candidates to bring 'actual parts' and that an engineer exploited a security bug to access confidential files suggests a systematic rather than incidental pattern of trade secret misappropriation.
